How I Got Here

The Unglamorous Part of the Car

I joined NER because it reminded me of FIRST — a team building toward one goal, with competition at the end. I landed in motor cooling almost by accident. The subteam needed people, and I raised my hand. Cooling isn't flashy. Nobody's talking about the swirl pot at the reveal. But the more I worked on it, the more I realized: if the motor overheats, the car stops. Past NER cars had heat issues at Michigan competitions where summer temperatures push every thermal margin to the edge. That's when it clicked — cooling isn't a detail, it's a constraint everything else is designed around.

What I Did
  • Rerouted tubing paths and optimized diameters — cut 2 lb from the cooling loop without touching thermal performance
  • Modeled the pump welding jig and swirl pot bracket in SolidWorks, designing for the tight packaging reality of a tube-frame chassis where nothing fits cleanly
Flow Meter Bench Test Setup Pump · turbine flow meter · radiator · tubing circuit
Test & Validate

You Don't Know Until You Measure

Designing something in CAD and knowing it works are two different things. I built a bench test circuit — pump, turbine flow meter, radiator, tubing — to actually measure what the system was doing versus what the spec sheet said it should do. The gap between those two numbers is where real engineering happens.

  • Compared rated vs. measured pump flow across multiple tubing configurations — data that directly informed which diameter and routing to commit to
  • Ran comparative thermal dissipation tests and logged failure modes at each iteration
  • Every result fed back into the next design decision — nothing got changed without a reason
Redesigned Swirl Pot Lid — SolidWorks Revised sealing surface · tighter tolerances · SLA printed
Problem Identified

We Saw Coolant on the Floor

During testing, coolant showed up where it wasn't supposed to be. The OEM swirl pot lid wasn't sealing. Once we actually looked at it, the mismatch was obvious — you could see it. I measured the original geometry against what the sealing face needed to be, and the tolerance stack-up was right there. The gap wasn't dramatic, but it didn't need to be. Any gap means air and coolant escaping, which means pressure loss, which means the system isn't doing its job.

  • Traced the failure to a tolerance issue on the sealing face — visible mismatch once the part was measured against spec
  • Redesigned the lid from scratch with corrected tolerances to close the gap entirely
  • Chose SLA over FDM specifically for surface finish — FDM layer lines don't seal, SLA does
Swirl Pot + Radiator — PDM Integration Mockup Positional assembly for master powertrain CAD
Systems Thinking

Your Part Lives Inside a Bigger System

One thing I've learned working on a car with a full team: your component doesn't exist in isolation. The swirl pot and radiator have to fit inside a chassis that other subteams are also designing around. If your geometry is wrong, you find out at assembly — and that's a bad time to find out. I built a positional mockup so our cooling components could be dropped into the master powertrain CAD via SolidWorks PDM before anything was built. Spatial conflicts caught in software cost an afternoon. Caught in metal, they cost a lot more.

  • Maintained shared CAD files through SolidWorks PDM so the whole team always worked from current geometry
  • Caught packaging conflicts virtually before they became fabrication problems

The Brief

Design for a User Who Won't Read Instructions

The constraint was simple and brutal: design a STEM toy for kindergarteners, build it in a makerspace, and spend under $100. The user testing was even more clarifying — five-year-olds do not care about your design intent. They interact with what's in front of them, and if it doesn't make sense immediately, they move on. That meant everything had to be intuitive by feel, not by explanation.

  • Led all SolidWorks modeling — full exploded assembly, individual part drawings, and 3D-printed snap-fit gear holders
  • Panel angle went through three iterations: 45° → 25° → 35° — each tested with real kids before the next was cut
  • The 35° final angle was a compromise: enough slope to keep marbles moving, not so steep they couldn't interact with the gears
Physical Prototype — 50% Complete Laser-cut wood + acrylic · assembled in makerspace
When the Users Fix Your Design For You

The Kids Were Right

At the first school visit, marbles kept getting stuck in the gears. The kids didn't complain — they just tried again, and sometimes it worked, sometimes it didn't. That inconsistency was the problem. We watched them play, took notes, and went back to the shop. The gear holders had square notches; the marble's path was creating a pinch point. We added a fillet to the entry geometry and the problem went away. At the final showcase, thirty kids played with it and it held up. Watching them figure it out — and genuinely enjoy it — was one of the better moments of the semester.

  • Laser-cut gears from 0.11" plywood; gear holders redesigned from square to filleted circular notch after live user observation
  • Integrated Arduino UNO, micro switches, LCD prompt system, and green LED scoring — all from a single SparkFun kit
  • Solved a firmware delay bug blocking simultaneous LCD + LED outputs
  • Total spend: $10 of a $100 budget — makerspace materials covered the rest

Where It Started

Everyone Had a Role and Trusted Each Other

FIRST Robotics was where I learned what a real engineering team actually feels like. Not everyone doing everything — everyone owning something specific, and trusting that the person next to them owned theirs. I started on mechanical, designing the intake and drivetrain from scratch in CAD. When 3D-printed parts failed under match loads, we didn't just reprint them — we figured out why the load path was wrong and redesigned. That habit of asking why before fixing stuck with me.

  • Designed intake, drivetrain, and double linear slide subsystem from scratch in CAD across multiple seasons
  • Identified structural failures in 3D-printed competition parts under load; traced root cause and redesigned geometry before reprinting
  • Fabricated and assembled custom mechanical components — often under time pressure with competition the next day

Understanding the Whole Robot

By the time I became Drive Coach, I'd already built the robot, driven it in matches, and watched it fail in ways I hadn't anticipated. That progression mattered. You can only do that well if you understand what the robot can and can't do under pressure. Building it first meant I knew exactly where its limits were.
